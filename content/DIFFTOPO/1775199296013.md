# Regular Values, Tangent Spaces and Transversality

Since $dg_p = dG_p$ is onto, we see that $\ker(dg_p)$ is a codimension $l$ subspace of $T_p U$ (by rank-nullity).

$$T_p U \xrightarrow{dg_p} T_a \mathbb{R}^l$$

Similarly, $\ker(d(\partial g)_p)$ is a codimension $l$ subspace of $T_p(\partial U)$.
$d(\partial g)_p$ is onto, therefore $\ker(d(\partial g)_p) \neq \ker(dg_p)$. But $d(\partial g)_p$ is the restriction of $dg_p$ to $T_p(\partial U)$. Thus:

$$\ker(dg_p) \nsubseteq T_p(\partial U)$$

Thus $S$ intersects $\partial \mathbb{H}^k$ transversally at $p$. 
(Since $T_p S + \mathbb{R}^{k-1} = \mathbb{R}^k$).

---

# Global Transversal Intersection

Since this is true $\forall p \in S \cap \partial \mathbb{H}^k$, $S$ intersects $\partial \mathbb{H}^k$ transversally everywhere. Hence $S \cap \mathbb{H}^k$ is a codimension $l$ manifold with boundary $S \cap \partial \mathbb{H}^k$ by above corollary. This shows that $g^{-1}(a)$ is a codimension $l$ manifold with boundary $g^{-1}(a) \cap \partial U$.

> [!INFO] Definition
> Transversality is defined the same way as before: for $f: X \to Y$, mapping $x \mapsto y \in Z$:
> 
> $$\operatorname{im}(df_x) + T_y Z = T_y Y$$
