We use the family $F$ as in the Proposition. By the **Transversal Family Theorem**, $f_s, \partial f_s$ are transversal to $Z$ for almost all $s \in S$.

## Motivation for ε-Neighbourhood Theorem

To prove the $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood theorem, we need to talk about the **normal bundle of $Y$ in $\mathbb{R}^M$**.

When $Y$ is not $\mathbb{R}^N$, we need a way to get back to $Y$ after translating.

> [!TIP] ε-Neighbourhood Theorem
> If $Y \subseteq \mathbb{R}^M$ is boundaryless, then $\exists$ a positive smooth function $\varepsilon: Y \to \mathbb{R}$ and a submersion $\pi: Y^\varepsilon \to Y$ which is identity on $Y$. If $Y$ is compact, then $\varepsilon$ can be chosen to be constant.
