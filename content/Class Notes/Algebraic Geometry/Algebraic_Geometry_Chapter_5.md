# Chapter 5: Projective and Birational Geometry

The first part of §5 aims to generalise the content of §§3–4 to projective varieties. The remainder is concerned with birational geometry.

## Why projective varieties?
Projective varieties provide a natural framework for studying varieties, avoiding the issues of "missing points" at infinity that occur in affine geometry (e.g., intersection of parallel lines).
Example: $\mathbb{P}^1$ is the union of two copies of $\mathbb{A}^1$ glued together by $x \mapsto 1/x$.

## Graded rings and homogeneous ideals

> [!INFO] Definition
> A polynomial $f \in k[X_0, \dots, X_n]$ is **homogeneous of degree $d$** if $f(\lambda X_0, \dots, \lambda X_n) = \lambda^d f(X_0, \dots, X_n)$ for all $\lambda$.

> [!INFO] Definition
> An ideal $I \subset k[X_0, \dots, X_n]$ is **homogeneous** if for all $f \in I$, the homogeneous components of $f$ are also in $I$.
> Equivalently, $I$ is generated by homogeneous polynomials.

## The homogeneous $V-I$ correspondences

Let $\mathbb{P}^n$ be the $n$-dimensional projective space.
For $f$ homogeneous, the condition $f(P) = 0$ is well-defined for $P \in \mathbb{P}^n$.

Correspondences:
$$ \{ \text{homogeneous ideals } J \} \xrightarrow{V} \{ \text{subsets } X \subset \mathbb{P}^n \} $$
$$ J \mapsto V(J) = \{ P \in \mathbb{P}^n \mid f(P) = 0 \quad \forall \text{ homogeneous } f \in J \} $$
$$ \{ \text{subsets } X \} \xrightarrow{I} \{ \text{homogeneous ideals } I \} $$
$$ X \mapsto I(X) = \{ f \in k[X_0, \dots, X_n] \mid f(P) = 0 \quad \forall P \in X \} $$

$V(I)$ defines algebraic subsets, forming the closed sets of the **Zariski topology** on $\mathbb{P}^n$.

## Projective Nullstellensatz

The ideal $(X_0, \dots, X_n)$ defines the empty set in $\mathbb{P}^n$ and is called the **irrelevant ideal**.

> [!TIP] Theorem (Projective Nullstellensatz)
> Assume $k$ is algebraically closed.
> (i) $V(J) = \emptyset \iff \text{rad } J \supset (X_0, \dots, X_n)$.
> (ii) If $V(J) \neq \emptyset$, then $I(V(J)) = \text{rad } J$.

> [!TIP] Corollary
> bijections between:
> \{homogeneous radical ideals $J \not\supset (X_0 \dots X_n)$\} $\leftrightarrow$ \{algebraic subsets\}
> \{homogeneous prime ideals $J \not\supset (X_0 \dots X_n)$\} $\leftrightarrow$ \{irreducible algebraic subsets\}

Affine cone over $V$: $V^a(J) \subset \mathbb{A}^{n+1}$.

## Rational functions on $V$

Let $V \subset \mathbb{P}^n$ be irreducible.
Elements of $k[X_0, \dots, X_n]$ are not functions on $V$ (unless constant).
We work with rational functions.

> [!INFO] Definition
> A **rational function** on $V$ is a function $f : V \dashrightarrow k$ given by $f(P) = g(P)/h(P)$, where $g, h$ are homogeneous of the same degree $d$, and $h \notin I(V)$.

Usually $k[V]$ (coordinate ring) isn't used for projective varieties in the same way.
The field of rational functions is $k(V)$.
Regularity at $P$: $f = g/h$ with $h(P) \neq 0$.
Local ring $\mathcal{O}_{V,P} = \{ f \in k(V) \mid f \text{ is regular at } P \}$.

## Affine covering of a projective variety

$\mathbb{P}^n$ is covered by $n+1$ standard affine pieces $U_i = \{ X_i \neq 0 \} \cong \mathbb{A}^n$.
$V_{(i)} = V \cap U_i$ is an affine algebraic set.
$k(V) \cong k(V_{(i)})$.

> [!TIP] Proposition
> Correspondence $V \mapsto V_{(0)}$ is a bijection between irreducible algebraic subsets $V \subset \mathbb{P}^n$ (not contained in hyperplane $X_0=0$) and irreducible algebraic subsets $V_0 \subset \mathbb{A}^n$.

## Rational maps and morphisms

Rational map $f : V \dashrightarrow \mathbb{P}^m$ given by $P \mapsto (f_0(P) : \dots : f_m(P))$ where $f_i \in k(V)$.
Regularity at $P$: exists expression with $f_i$ regular and at least one $f_i(P) \neq 0$.

> [!INFO] Definition
> A **morphism** $f : V \to W$ is a rational map regular at every point of $V$.

## Examples

### Rational normal curve
$f : \mathbb{P}^1 \to \mathbb{P}^m$ by $(U:V) \mapsto (U^m : U^{m-1}V : \dots : V^m)$.
Image is defined by rank $\begin{pmatrix} X_0 & \dots & X_{m-1} \\ X_1 & \dots & X_m \end{pmatrix} \le 1$.

### Linear projection
$\pi : \mathbb{P}^3 \dashrightarrow \mathbb{P}^2$ by $(X_0 : \dots : X_3) \mapsto (X_1 : X_2 : X_3)$.
Defined outside $P_0 = (1:0:0:0)$.

## Birational maps

> [!INFO] Definition
> $f : V \dashrightarrow W$ is **birational** if it has a rational inverse.

> [!TIP] Proposition
> Equivalent conditions:
> (i) $f$ is birational.
> (ii) $f$ dominant and $f^* : k(W) \to k(V)$ is isomorphism.
> (iii) Exists open $V_0 \subset V$ and $W_0 \subset W$ such that $f|_{V_0} : V_0 \to W_0$ is isomorphism.

### Rational varieties
$V$ is **rational** if $k(V)$ is purely transcendental (isomorphic to $k(t_1, \dots, t_n)$).
Equivalently, $V$ contains a dense open set isomorphic to an open set of $\mathbb{A}^n$.

## Reduction to a hypersurface
Every variety is birational to a hypersurface (using primitive element/Noether normalisation).

## Products
$V \times W$.
Affine case: $V \times W \subset \mathbb{A}^{n+m}$ is a variety.
Projective case: Need to embed $\mathbb{P}^n \times \mathbb{P}^m$ into some $\mathbb{P}^N$.

### Segre embedding
$\phi : \mathbb{P}^n \times \mathbb{P}^m \to \mathbb{P}^N$ where $N = (n+1)(m+1)-1$.
Coords $U_{ij} = X_i Y_j$.
Image $\Sigma_{n,m}$ defined by rank condition on matrix $(U_{ij})$.
If $V \subset \mathbb{P}^n, W \subset \mathbb{P}^m$, then $V \times W \subset \mathbb{P}^n \times \mathbb{P}^m \cong \Sigma_{n,m} \subset \mathbb{P}^N$ is a projective variety.
