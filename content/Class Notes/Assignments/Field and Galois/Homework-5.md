6. Let $k$ be a field, $\operatorname{char}(k) = 0$, $f(x) \in k[x]$ be a polynomial of degree $n$; $a, h \in k$. Prove that
$$
f(a + h) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{1}{k!} f^{(k)}(a) h^k,
$$
where
$$
f^{(k)}(x) = \left(f^{(k-1)}(x)\right)', \quad f^{(1)}(x) = f'(x).
$$
**Answer:** We can prove this by induction on the degree of the polynomial. For $n=0$, $f(x) = c$ for some constant $c \in k$. Then $f(a+h) = c$ and the formula holds trivially. For $n=1$, $f(x) = mx + b$. Then $f(a+h) = m(a+h) + b = ma + mh + b$. The formula gives:
$$
f(a+h) = f(a) + f'(a)h = (ma + b) + m h, 
$$
which matches. Assume the formula holds for polynomials of degree $n-1$. Let $f(x)$ be a polynomial of degree $n$. We can write:
$$
f(x) = (x - a)g(x) + f(a),
$$
where $g(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $n-1$. Then:
$$
f(a+h) = (h)g(a+h) + f(a)
$$
By the induction hypothesis, we have:
$$
g(a+h) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{k!} g^{(k)}(a) h^k.
$$
Substituting this back, we get:
$$
f(a+h) = h \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{k!} g^{(k)}(a) h^k + f(a) = f(a) + \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{k!} g^{(k)}(a) h^{k+1}.
$$
Now, we need to relate $g^{(k)}(a)$ to $f^{(k+1)}(a)$. By Libniz's rule, we have:
$$
f^{(k+1)}(x) = (x-a)g^{(k+1)}(x) + (k+1)g^{(k)}(x).
$$
Evaluating at $x=a$, we get:
$$
f^{(k+1)}(a) = (k+1)g^{(k)}(a).
$$
Thus, $g^{(k)}(a) = \frac{f^{(k+1)}(a)}{k+1}$. Substituting this back into our expression for $f(a+h)$, we get:
$$
f(a+h) = f(a) + \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{k!} \frac{f^{(k+1)}(a)}{k+1} h^{k+1} = f(a) + \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{1}{(j-1)! j} f^{(j)}(a) h^j.
$$
Reindexing the sum, we have:
$$
f(a+h) = f(a) + \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{1}{j!} f^{(j)}(a) h^j.
$$
Combining the terms, we get:
$$
f(a+h) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{1}{k!} f^{(k)}(a) h^k,
$$
which completes the induction step and proves the formula for all polynomials of degree .$\quad \blacksquare$

8. Let $L/k$ be a finite extension, $[L : k] = n$. Let $E/k$ be any field extension. Prove that the number of distinct $k$-embeddings of $L$ into $E$ is at most $n$.
**Answer:** So we can construct a tower as,
$$
k \subseteq k(\alpha_{1}) \subseteq k(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2}) \subseteq \dots k(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2},\dots,\alpha_{n})= L
$$
With,
$$
[k(\alpha_{1}):k][k(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2}):k(\alpha_{1})]\dots[k(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2},\dots,\alpha_{n}):k(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2},\dots,\alpha_{n-1})]=n
$$
Now start with,
$$
\sigma :k \hookrightarrow L
$$
Then we can extend this to $k(\alpha_{1})$ in at most $[k(\alpha_{1}):k]$ many ways. So on each of these we can extend to $k(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2})$ in at most $[k(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2}):k(\alpha_{1})]$ many ways. Continuing this way we get that the total number of extensions is at most n.

9. Let $f(x) \in k[x]$ be a monic polynomial with distinct roots $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$, $\deg f = n$. Prove
$$
f'(x) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \prod_{i \neq j} (x - \alpha_i).
$$

**Answer:** We only need to prove product rule for polynomials and then we can use induction on the degree of the polynomial to prove the above statement. For two polynomials $g(x)$ and $h(x)$ we have,
$$
(g(x)h(x))' = g'(x)h(x) + g(x)h'(x)
$$
We can prove this by induction on the degree of $g(x)$. For $\deg(g) = 0$, $g(x) = c$ for some constant $c \in k$. Then $(g(x)h(x))' = (ch(x))' = c h'(x)$ and the formula holds trivially. For $\deg(g) = 1$, $g(x) = mx + b$. Then:
$$
(g(x)h(x))' = (mx + b)h'(x) + m h(x),
$$
which matches the product rule. Assume the formula holds for polynomials of degree $n-1$. Let $g(x)$ be a polynomial of degree $n$. We can write:
$$
g(x) = (x - a)q(x) + g(a),
$$
where $q(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $n-1$. Then:
$$
\begin{gather*}
(g(x)h(x))' = \Big(((x-a)q(x)+g(a))h(x)  \Big)' \\
= ((x-a)q(x)h(x))' + (g(a)h(x))' \\
= ((x-a)q(x)h(x))' + g(a)h'(x) \\
= q(x)[(x-a)h(x)]' +q'(x)[(x-a)h(x)]  g(a)h'(x) \\
= q(x)[h(x) + (x-a)h'(x)] + q'(x)(x-a)h(x) + g(a)h'(x) \\
= q(x)h(x) + (x-a)q(x)h'(x) + q'(x)(x-a)h(x) + g(a)h'(x) \\ 
= g'(x)h(x) + g(a)h'(x)
\end{gather*}
$$
Now the above formula follows.
