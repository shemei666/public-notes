### Problem 1

Let $(X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)$ be a measure space, and $\{E_n\}$ be a sequence in $\mathcal{M}$. Show that,

(a) $\mu(\liminf E_n) \le \liminf \mu(E_n)$.

Applying **Fatou's Lemma** to $\{\chi_{E_{n}}\}$ we have,
$$
\int \lim \inf \chi_{E_{n}} d\mu \leq \lim \inf \int \chi_{E_{n}}d\mu  
$$
Let $L =\lim \inf E_{n}$, then we have $\lim \inf \chi_{E_{n}} = \chi_{L}$. Since,
$$
\lim \inf \chi_{E_{n}}(x) = \sup_{k} \inf_{n\geq k} \chi_{E_{n}}(x) = \begin{cases}
1  & x \in \lim \inf E_{n} \\
0  &  \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
= \chi_{L}
$$

$$
\implies \mu(L) = \mu (\lim \inf E_{n}) \leq \lim \inf \mu(E_{n})
$$

---

(b) If there exists $A \in \mathcal{M}$ such that $\mu(A) < \infty$ and $E_n \subseteq A$ for every $n \in \mathbb{N}$, then $\mu(\limsup E_n) \ge \limsup \mu(E_n)$.

Consider the sequence $C_{n} = A- E_{n}$ then we have $\mu(C_{n})=\mu(A)-\mu(E)$, since $E_{n}\subseteq A$ and $\mu(A)<\infty$.
Also, 
$$
\begin{align}
\lim \inf  C_{n} =\lim \inf(A-E_{n})  & = \bigcup_{n\geq{1}}^{}\bigcap_{j\geq n}^{}(A-E_{j}) \\
 & = \bigcup_{n\geq 1}^{}\left( A - \bigcup_{j\geq n}^{}E_{n} \right) = A - \bigcap_{n>1}^{}\bigcup_{j\geq n}^{}E_{n}  \\
 & = A - \lim \sup E_{n}
\end{align}
$$
Hence we have by (a)
$$
\begin{align}
\mu(A) - \mu(\lim \sup E_{n})  & =\mu(A - \lim \sup E_{n}) \\
 & =\mu(\lim \inf C_{n})  \\
 & \leq \lim \inf \mu(C_{n})   \tag{using (a)}\\
 & = \lim \inf\left[\mu(A)-\mu(E_{n})\right] \\
 \implies \mu(\lim \sup E_{n})  & \leq \lim \sup \mu(E_{n})
\end{align}
$$

### Problem 2

Consider $\mathcal{M} = \{E \subseteq \mathbb{R}: E$ is countable or $E^c$ is countable}. Show that,

(a) $\mathcal{M}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra.

1. If $E \in \mathcal{M}$, then $\lvert E \rvert = \lvert \mathbb{N} \rvert$ or $\lvert E^{c} \rvert= \lvert \mathbb{N} \rvert$ $\implies$ $\lvert E^{c} \rvert=\lvert \mathbb{N} \rvert$ or $\lvert (E^{c})^{c} \rvert =\lvert \mathbb{N} \rvert$ , since $(E^{c})^{c} = E$. Hence $E^{c} \in \mathcal{M}$
2. Now to check it is closed under countable union, Let $\{ E_{n} \}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ be a collection of sets in $\mathcal{M}$, If all of $E_{n}$ are countable then $\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} E_{n}$ is countable as countable union of countable sets are countable. Now if atleast one of $E_{n}$ is not countable, say $E_{1}$ is not countable, implies $E_{1}^{c}$ is countable $\implies \left( \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}E_{n} \right)^{c}=\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}E_{n}^{c} \subseteq E_{1}^{c}$ Hence $\left( \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}E_{n} \right)^{c}$ is countable. So $\mathcal{M}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra. $\quad \blacksquare$

---

(b) $\mathcal{M} \subsetneq \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}$.
Let $E \in \mathcal{M}$ WLOG assume that $E$ is countable, otherwise take its complement, since $\mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}$ is a sigma-algebra if $E\subseteq \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}} \implies E^{c}\subseteq \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}$.  Now since $E$ is assumed to be countable we have $E = \{ x_{1},x_{2},\dots, \}$ an enumeration of $E$. We now define
$$
E_{n} = \{ x_{1},x_{2},\dots,x_{n} \} \quad \forall n \in \mathbb{N}
$$
We see that $E_{n}$ is closed in $\mathbb{R},\ \forall n\in \mathbb{N}$. Then $E = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}E_{n}$ is a countable union of closed sets in $\mathbb{R}$ hence $E \in\mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}$.
Now see that $(0,1) \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}$ but $(0,1)$ is not countable neither is $(0,1)^{c}=(\infty,0]\cup[1,\infty)$. So $(0,1)$ is not in $\mathcal{M}$. Hence have shown $\mathcal{M} \subsetneq \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}} \quad \blacksquare$

---

(c) $\mathcal{M}$ is generated by $\{\{x\}: x \in \mathbb{R}\}$.

Given $E \in \mathcal{M}$ we have that $E$ is countable or $E^{c}$ is countable. WLOG we assume $E$ is countable else we take its complement, since showing the complement $E^{c}$ is generated by $\{ \{ x \}:x \in \mathbb{R} \}$ implies the set $E$ is also generated by it . Then,
$$
\begin{gather}
E = \{ x_{1},x_{2},\dots, \} \text{ be an enumeration} \\
E = \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty }\{x_{i}\}

$$
Hence $\mathcal{M}$ is generated by $\{ \{ x \}:x \in \mathbb{R} \} \quad \blacksquare$

### Problem 3

Let $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{L}, m)$ be the Lebesgue measure space and $E \in \mathcal{L}$ such that $m(E) > 0$. Prove that, $\exists F \subseteq E$ such that $F \notin \mathcal{L}$.

We can partition $E \in \mathcal{L}$ as follows
$$
E = \bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}^{} E \cap [n,n+1)
$$
The elements in the RHS are Lebesgue measurable since both $E$ and $[n,n+1)$ are Lebesgue measurable. Hence $m(E) = \sum_{n\in \mathbb{Z}}^{} m\left(E \cap [n,n+1)\right) >0$ Hence one of the terms in the sum must be non-zero, call it $E_{0}= E \cap [n_{0},n_{0}+1)$. We now partition the elements of $E_{0}$ using the equivalence relation
$$
x \sim y \iff x-y \in \mathbb{Q} 
$$
Now by Axiom of choice we can choose one element from each equivalence class under $\sim$, call it $V$. Also let $\mathbb{Q}\cap[-1,1] = \{ q_{1},q_{2},\dots  \}$ be an enumeration of rationals in $[-1,1]$. We now define
$$
V_{k}:= V + q_{k}
$$
Now since $E_{0}\subseteq [n_{0},n+1)$ ,$E_{0}$ can be made by taking all shifts of $V$ by rational values in $[-1,1]$.
$$
\begin{gather}
E_{0} \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} V_{k} \subseteq [n_{0}-1,n_{0}+2] \\
0<m(E_{0}) \leq \sum_{k=1}^{\infty } m(V_{k}) \leq 3
\end{gather}
$$
We use the property of Lebesgue measure that it is invariant under shifts. Hence $m(V_{k}) = m(V)$.
$$
0<m(E_{0})\leq \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} m(V) \leq 3
$$
This gives a contradiction since if $m(V)>0$ implies $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} m(V)=\infty$ and if $m(V)=0$ implies $0<\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}m(V)=0 \quad \blacksquare$

### Problem 5

Let $f(x) = x^2$ on $\mathbb{R}$ and $E \subseteq [0, \infty)$. Show that, $m^*(E) = 0$ if and only if $m^*(f(E)) = 0$, where $m^*$ is the Lebesgue outer measure on $\mathbb{R}$.

$(\implies)$ $m^{*}(E)= 0$ implies $\forall \varepsilon >0,\exists \{(a_{i},b_{i})\}_{i=1}^{\infty }$ such that $E \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}(a_{i},b_{i})$ and $\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} (b_{i}-a_{i}) < \varepsilon$. 
We partition $E$ as $E = \bigcup_{n=0}^{\infty }E\cap [n,n+1)$, since $m^{*}(E)$ so is $m^{*}(E \cap [n,n+1))=0$. So we can find $\{ (a_{i,n},b_{i,n})\}$ such that
$$
\begin{gather}
E \cap [n,n+1) \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} [a_{i,n},b_{i,n}) \quad b>a_{i,n}\geq {n} \\
\sum_{i=1}^{\infty } b_{i,n} - a_{i,n} < \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{n+2}(n+1)} \\
\implies \sum_{i=1}^{\infty } b_{i,n}^{2} - a_{i,n}^{2} = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty } (b_{i,n}-a_{i,n})(b_{i,n}+a_{i,n}) \\
\leq 2(n+1) \sum_{i=1}^{\infty } (b_{i,n}-a_{i,n}) <\frac{\varepsilon}{2^{n+1}}
\end{gather}
$$
Then $\bigcup_{n=0}^{\infty}\{ (a_{i,n},b_{i,n}) \}_{i=1}^{\infty}$ is a cover for $E$ and hence $\bigcup_{n=0}^{\infty} \{ (a_{i,n}^{2},b_{i,n}^{2}) \}_{i=1}^{\infty}$ is a cover for $f(E)$ but from above we get that,
$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty }\sum_{i=1}^{\infty } (b_{i,n}^{2}-a_{i,n}^{2}) < \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{n+1}} = \varepsilon 
$$
Hence $m^{*}(E^{2}) = 0$.
$(\impliedby )$ We first split $E$ as $f(E)= f(E)\cap [0,1] \cup f(E)\cap (1,\infty)$, so we get that $m^{*}(f(E)\cap [0,1])=0$ and $m^{*}(f(E)\cap(1,\infty ))=0$. 

Let $A = f(E)\cap[0,1],B=f(E)\cap(1,\infty )$. Now we find a cover $[a_{i},b_{i}]$ of $A$ such that $\sum_{}^{}b_{i}-a_{i}<\varepsilon$ for $\varepsilon>0$. Let $n_{0} \in \mathbb{N}$ be such that $\frac{1}{n_{0}} < \frac{\varepsilon^{2}}{4}$ . Also since $m^{*}\left( \left[ \frac{1}{n} ,1\right]\cap f(E) \right)=0$ so we have a cover of it such that
$$
\begin{gather}
f(E) \cap \left[ \frac{1}{n} ,1\right] \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}\left[c_{i,n},d_{i,n}\right) \quad c_{i,n}\geq \frac{1}{n}\\
\sum_{i=1}^{\infty } d_{i,n} - c_{i,n} < \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{n+1}\sqrt{ n }} \\
\implies \sum_{i=1} ^{\infty } \left(\sqrt{ d_{i,n} } -\sqrt{ c_{i,n} }\right)   \leq \frac{\sqrt{ n }}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{\infty } d_{i,n} - c_{i,n} < \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{n+2}}
\end{gather}
$$
The last implication is because of the Lipschitz continuity of $f^{-1} = \sqrt{ x }$ on the interval $\left[ \frac{1}{n} ,\infty \right)$, this gives $|\sqrt{ x }-\sqrt{ y }| \leq \frac{\sqrt{ n }}{2} \lvert x-y \rvert$ for $x,y \in [\frac{1}{n}, \infty)$
Now we have a cover of $f(E)$ which gives us a cover of $E$ by applying $f^{-1}=\sqrt{ x }$ to the cover.
$$
\begin{gather}
\left\{  \left[0, \frac{\varepsilon^{2}}{4} \right)  \right\} \cup \bigcup_{n\leq n_{0}}^{} \{ \left[c_{i,n},d_{i,n}\right) \}_{i=1}^{\infty } \text{ is a cover of }f(E) \\
\implies \left\{  \left[0, \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \right)  \right\} \cup \bigcup_{n\leq n_{0}}^{} \left\{ \left[\sqrt{ c_{i,n} },\sqrt{ d_{i,n} }\right) \right\}_{i=1}^{\infty } \text{ is a cover of E}
\end{gather}
$$
Now we have
$$
\sqrt{ \frac{\varepsilon^{2}}{4} } + \sum_{n\leq n_{0}}^{} \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} (\sqrt{ d_{i,n} }-\sqrt{ c_{i,n} }) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \sum_{n\leq n_{0}}^{} \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{n+2}} < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}+ \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon 
$$
Hence $m^{*}(f^{-1}(A))=0$. Now if $\{ [a_{i},b_{i})\}$ is a cover of $B$ with $\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} b_{i} - a_{i} < \varepsilon$ then $\{ [\sqrt{ a_{i}},\sqrt{ b_{i} } ] \}$ is a cover of $f^{-1}(B)$ such that
$$
\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \sqrt{ b_{i} } -\sqrt{ a_{i} } \leq \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} b_{i} - a_{i} < \varepsilon \quad\text{(same Lipschitz prop. used above)}
$$
Implies $m^{*}(B)=0$, hence $m^{*}(A)=m^{*}(B)=0$ implies $m^{*}(E) = 0 \quad \blacksquare$

### Problem 7

Let $f,g: [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be an $\mathcal{L}$-measurable function with $\int f~dm = \int g~dm$. Show that either $f = g$ $m$-almost everywhere or there is a measurable set $E \subseteq [a,b]$ such that $\int_E f~dm > \int_E g~dm$.

Assume there is no measurable $E\subseteq [a,b]$ such that $\int_{E}f dm > \int_{E} g dm$ then we prove that $h:= f- g = 0$ almost everywhere.  See that $C =h^{-1}((0,\infty ))$ is a measurable set hence we get that 
$$
f(x)-g(x) > 0 , x \in C
$$
Hence $C$ has to be measure 0 otherwise,
$$
\int_{C} f > \int_{C}g
$$
Now for $D = h^{-1}(-\infty,0)$ is measurable set and if $D$ is measure zero we are done, otherwise we see that,

$$
\int_{D} f < \int_{D}g
$$
$D^{c}$ cannot be measure zero since $\int_{D}f + \int_{D^{c}}f = \int_{D}g + \int_{D^{c}}g$.

$$
\implies \int_{D^{c}} f > \int_{D^{c}} g  \quad \text{contradiction}
$$

Hence $D$ has to be measure zero, so $h=0$ almost everywhere.$\quad \blacksquare$

### Problem 8

Let $(X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)$ be a measure space and $\{f_n: X \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\}$ be a sequence of non-negative $\mathcal{M}$-measurable functions such that $f_n \rightarrow f$ $\mu$-almost everywhere and $\int f_n d\mu \rightarrow \int f~d\mu$. Show that for each $E \in \mathcal{M}$, $\int_E f_n d\mu \rightarrow \int_E f~d\mu$.

Consider $\lvert f - f_{n} \rvert$, using Triangle inequality we have that $\lvert f \rvert+\lvert f_{n} \rvert-\lvert f-f_{n} \rvert\geq {0}$ hence we can apply **Fatou's lemma** to get,
$$
\begin{gather}
\int \lim \inf (\lvert f \rvert  + \lvert f_{n} \rvert - \lvert f- f_{n} \rvert ) < \lim \inf \int \lvert  f \rvert  + \lvert f_{n} \rvert - \lvert  f - f_{n}\rvert \\
\implies \int \lvert f \rvert  + \lvert f \rvert \leq \int \lvert f \rvert + \int \lvert f_{n} \rvert -\lim \sup \int \lvert f- f_{n} \rvert \\
\implies 2\int \lvert f \rvert  \leq 2\int \lvert f \rvert  - \lim \sup \int \lvert f - f_{n} \rvert  \\
\implies \lim \sup \int \lvert f - f_{n} \rvert \leq 0 \\
\implies \int \lvert f - f_{n} \rvert \to 0
\end{gather}
$$
Now we can use this to show that for any measurable $E$ and assuming the sequence $\{ f_{n} \}$ is made of integrable functions.
$$
\left\lvert  \int_{E} \lvert f \rvert -\int_{E}\lvert f_{n} \rvert  \right\rvert \leq \int_{E} \lvert f-f_{n} \rvert \leq \int  \lvert f - f_{n} \rvert \to 0
$$
Hence we have that,
$$
\int_{E} f_{n} d\mu \to \int_{E} f d\mu \quad \blacksquare
$$

### Problem 9

Let $(X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)$ be a measure space with $\mu(X) < \infty$. Show that a non-negative $\mathcal{M}$-measurable function $f$ on $X$ is integrable if and only if the series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \mu(\{x: f(x) \ge n\})$ converges.

**Answer:**
$(\implies )$  Let $C_{n} = f^{-1}([n,n+1))$ we define $g$ as follows,
$$
\begin{gather}
g:= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty }n\chi_{C_{n}} \\
\int gd\mu = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty }n\mu(\chi_{C_{n}}) \\
= \underbrace{ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty }\sum_{m=0}^{n-1}\mu(\chi_{c_{n}}) =\sum_{m=0}^{\infty }\sum_{n=m+1}^{\infty } \mu(\chi_{{C_{n}}}) }_{ \text{sums can be switched since the terms are non-negative} }= \sum_{m=0}^{\infty } \mu(\{ x:f(x)\geq m +1\})  \\
g\leq f \implies \sum_{m=0}^{\infty } \mu(\{ x:f(x)\geq m +1 \}) \leq \int f d\mu < \infty \\
\implies \sum_{m=0}^{\infty } \mu(\{ x:f(x)\geq m \}) < \infty
\end{gather}
$$
$(\impliedby )$ Similarly we get,
$$
\begin{gather}
h:= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (n+1)\chi_{C_{n}} \\
\int h d\mu = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty } (n+1) \mu(\chi_{C_{n}})  \\
= \underbrace{ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty } \sum_{m=0}^{n} \mu(\chi_{C_{n}}) =\sum_{m=0}^{\infty } \sum_{n=m}^{\infty } \mu(\chi_{C_{n}}) }_{ \text{sums can be switched since terms are non-negative} }= \sum_{m=0}^{\infty } \mu(\{ x:f(x)\geq m \}) < \infty \\
f \leq h \implies \int f d\mu \leq \int h d\mu < \infty 
\end{gather}
$$
Hence $f$ is integrable.$\quad \blacksquare$

### Problem 10

Since $\{ f_{n} \}$ is bounded and converges uniformly so we have that $\lvert f_{n} \rvert< M$ and $\int M d\mu = M \mu(X)<\infty$, hence by DCT we have,
$$
\lim_{ n \to \infty } \int f_{n} d\mu = \int f d\mu
